EVP Reality Check

For each statement, choose the answer that’s most true today. This is a reality check: whether your EVP is clear, credible, and usable in daily work.

1) Clarity

1a. Our EVP can be explained in one minute without slides.

1b. Our EVP is specific to us, not interchangeable with competitors.

1c. People can describe what this EVP means in practice, not only in words.

2) Credibility

2a. Employees would recognize the EVP as mostly true today.

2b. We can point to multiple current examples that support each EVP theme.

2c. Our EVP names the tradeoffs and the hard parts of working here, not just the attractive parts.

3) Usability for managers

3a. Managers can use the EVP in hiring interviews tomorrow.

3b. Managers can use it in performance and feedback conversations tomorrow.

3c. Managers have concrete examples of what fits and what doesn’t.

4) Leadership signals

4a. Leadership choices reinforce the EVP under pressure.

4b. If leaders contradict the EVP, it gets noticed and corrected.

4c. The EVP helps leaders make tradeoffs, not just statements.

5) Work environment

5a. Most teams can work in a way that fits the EVP without relying on heroics or constant firefighting.

5b. Most employees experience the EVP themes in daily work and not just in stories or exceptions.

5c. Across teams, departments and/or countries the EVP holds up without escalation and negotiation becoming the default way work gets done.

6) Rewards and recognition

6a. Promotion and recognition decisions reinforce the EVP themes.

6b. We do not reward the opposite behavior 'because results'.

6c. Our KPIs and targets don’t routinely force tradeoffs that contradict the EVP themes.

7) Launch readiness

7a. We know what we will stop doing or stop saying because of this EVP.

7b. We have 2–3 simple proof signals to track whether it’s landing.

7c. A business leader co-owns EVP credibility after launch.
